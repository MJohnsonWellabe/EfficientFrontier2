Scenario Inspector

1 · Purpose & Overall Design

This tool searches for the capital-efficient mix of new business across three lines — Medicare Supplement (MS), Preneed (PN), and Hospital Indemnity (HI) — by re-running a full embedded-value (EV) projection under many candidate sales plans and locating the non-dominated set in risk/return space.

Each candidate scenario sets a 2026 new-business sales level for each product. For that scenario the tool rebuilds the seriatim EV cash flows, recomputes RBC/surplus, derives a set of return and capital metrics, tests them against the constraint suite, and plots the scenario on two axes: return (deterministic PVDE of 2026 issues) and downside risk (the CTE-90 shortfall of that PVDE versus the deterministic plan, across stochastic claim/termination draws). Feasible, non-dominated scenarios form the efficient frontier.

Issue-year focus. The return and capital-adequacy constraints and the frontier return/risk axes are evaluated on the 2026 issue year only — i.e. policies newly written in 2026, excluding the pre-2026 in-force block. This isolates the economics of the capital actually being deployed in the decision year. The VNB-by-product tab and the RBC/surplus projection continue to reflect all issue years and the full book; only the constraint and frontier metrics are narrowed to 2026 issues.

2 · VNB Engine — Income Statement Construction

The engine builds a monthly distributable-earnings statement per issue-year cohort, then aggregates to calendar years. Both the original and every scenario projection run on a 360-month (30-year) horizon, months 0 (Dec 2025) through 359 (Nov 2055). The annual rollup sums months by calendar year exactly as the source workbook's VNB Recalc does.

Line definitions ($, per cohort, summed across cohorts for portfolio views):

  • Premium = EarnedPrem − ReinsPrem (net of reinsurance).
  • Net investment income (NII) = mid-period average assets × monthly earned rate, where assets = CLRes + TabRes + TS and the monthly rate = (1 + NIER)1/12 − 1. NIER is per-product; the 2030 value is held flat for all later years.
  • Claims = IncClaims − ReinsClaims.
  • Other benefits = −(change in tabular reserve) − (change-in-loading), where the change-in-loading term is carried on Preneed; other products carry the ceded ALR reserve movement. This term is included in the recalc (it is a real reserve cost, not a presentation artifact).
  • Commission & premium tax scale with premium.
  • Acquisition = lives issued × per-life acquisition cost; inflated after the inflation-start year.
  • Maintenance = lives in-force × per-policy annual cost ÷ 12; inflated similarly. The 2030 unit cost applies from 2030 onward (pre-inflation base).
  • Pre-tax income (PTI) = revenue − benefits − expenses. Tax at the statutory rate. After-tax income (ATI) = PTI − tax.
  • Change in target surplus (ΔTS) is released/charged as required capital runs off.
  • Distributable earnings (DE) = ATI + ΔTS. This is the cash flow discounted for PVDE and solved for IRR.

IRR / PVDE. PVDE discounts the annual DE stream at the configured discount rate. IRR solves NPV(DE) = 0. Because a runoff DE stream can turn negative again in late years (terminal reserve release), the NPV function can have more than one sign change; the solver gathers every bracketed root and returns the one nearest a 10% guess, matching Excel's IRR convention and avoiding spurious deep-negative roots.

3 · EV Recalculation Under a Scenario

A scenario is defined by, per product: a 2026 new-business sales level, a claims scalar, and a lapse scalar. From these the engine derives a per-issue-year sales scalar and rebuilds lives and dollars cohort by cohort. Frontier scenarios grow the 2026 level by the sales-growth schedule; a custom scenario may instead supply an explicit per-year sales plan (2026–2035), which is used verbatim with no growth applied on top.

Sales scalar (per issue year Y): ss(Y) = updatedSales(Y) / originalSales(Y). Updated sales is the scenario sales level applied to every projection year; original sales is the workbook's per-year anchor for that issue year. Because the original-sales anchor varies by year, the scalar can differ year to year even when the scenario sales level is flat. Issue years beyond the scalar table (post-2035) and the pre-2026 in-force block receive a scalar of 1.

Lives in-force, two regimes:

  • Build regime (calendar year < issue year + 2): new cohorts scale proportionally, newLIF = origLIF × ss; the pre-2026 in-force block uses an additive form, newLIF = origLIF − origIssued + newIssued.
  • Persistency regime (calendar year ≥ issue year + 2): rolling year-over-year — each month references the prior recalc year, not a fixed base:
    newLIF[t] = newLIF[t−12] × max(0, 1 − (1 − origLIF[t]/origLIF[t−12]) × lapseScalar) × (ss for pre-2026 block; 1 for new cohorts)
    The rolling form is what the source workbook uses. It correctly compounds the lapse shock each year and avoids a divide-by-zero when a cohort's base month has zero in-force. Retention is floored at 0 but not capped at 1 (a cohort can grow during build-up).

Dollar & reserve variables rescale per surviving life: newVal[t] = origVal[t] / origLIF[t] × newLIF[t]. Claims additionally multiply by the claims scalar. This preserves the original per-life economics while re-weighting by the scenario's surviving lives. When a base month has zero in-force (tail months), the value passes through unscaled.

4 · RBC & Surplus

Target-surplus (TS) charges are loaded seriatim by product, line, and year-end date. The NAIC after-covariance required capital is:

PostCov = TSC0 + TSC4a + √((TSC1+TSLR016+TSC3)² + TSC1CS² + TSC2² + TSC4b²)

Required capital = PostCov × 1.03. Total Adjusted Capital (TAC) = Total Surplus − non-insurance portion + AVR + scenario income delta. RBC ratio = TAC / Required capital.

Charge rescaling under a scenario — by driver. Each modeled-product charge rescales by the recalc/original ratio of its economic driver, evaluated at year-end (month 12 for 2026, 24 for 2027, …):

  • TSC2 (the C-2 / insurance-risk charge) scales by the incurred-claims ratio (recalc IncClaims ÷ original IncClaims). Because claims carry the claims scalar that lives do not, this ratio differs from the lives ratio — using the lives ratio here understates or overstates TSC2 by the claims-scalar gap. This matches the workbook's Surplus Recalc formula for TSC2 exactly.
  • All other charges (TSC0, TSC1, TSLR016, TSC1CS, TSC3, TSC4a, TSC4b) scale by the lives-in-force ratio (recalc LivesInForce1 ÷ original LivesInForce1).
  • All-Other (non-modeled) charges are frozen at baseline.

TAC income delta (cumulative roll-forward). Scenario TAC is built year over year rather than as a single-year add-on. The seed year is baseline TAC plus the full-book pre-tax income delta (recalc − original, summed across the three products). Each later year carries the prior year's recalc TAC, adds the change in baseline TAC, and adds that year's income delta — taken pre-tax when baseline TAC declined that year, otherwise after-tax. Deltas are full-book (new business + pre-2026 in-force). This mirrors the source workbook's Surplus Recalc TAC formula.

Surplus note (default ON, $100M). An optional surplus note raises capital: the company receives the amount at the start date (less an upfront fee) and earns monthly investment income on the proceeds (at the note's investment-income rate, from after issue through maturity), pays quarterly interest — one quarter of the annual coupon every three months after issue, including the maturity quarter — and repays principal at maturity. The note's net cash flow accumulates into Total Adjusted Capital (it touches the model only through TAC, nowhere else), so the RBC ratio is re-derived from the note-adjusted TAC. Defaults: $100M, 10-year tenor, 9% interest, 3% upfront fee, 4% investment income, 2026-06-30 start.

5 · Constraints

A scenario is feasible when it satisfies every constraint below. Constraints C3–C8 are evaluated on 2026 issues only (pre-2026 in-force excluded); C1, C2, and C9 (trough-RBC tail) use the full projected capital position.

  • C1 — Minimum RBC ratio (default 4.0×): the minimum projected RBC ratio over 2026–2030 must be at least the floor. Uses the full book / all issue years.
  • C2 — Change in TAC / BOP TAC (default −12%): the year-over-year change in Total Adjusted Capital divided by beginning-of-period TAC, (TAC[y] − TAC[y−1]) / TAC[y−1], must be ≥ the floor in every year. It is applied in every year — the worst single-year TAC change must clear the floor.
  • C3 — 2026-issue IRR vs target (enforced): the IRR of the 2026 issue-year DE stream must be ≥ the sales-weighted average of the product hurdle rates, weighted by the scenario's 2026 sales mix.
  • C4 — 2026-issue IRR tail risk (a = 8%, b = 15%): across the stochastic claim/termination draws, P(2026-issue IRR < a) must be < b. This is the most volatility-sensitive constraint and requires a sufficient draw count (minimum 50, default 100) for a stable tail estimate.
  • C5 — 2026-issue DE positive by year (default yr 4 = 2029): the 2026 issue-year distributable earnings, summed across all three products, must be positive by the chosen projection year.
  • C6 — 2026-issue cumulative DE positive by year (default yr 10 = 2035): cumulative 2026-issue DE (all three products) must turn positive by the chosen year.
  • C7 — CumDE floor (default −$180M): the minimum cumulative 2026-issue DE (all three products) must not fall below the floor in any year — the deepest capital drawdown the 2026 cohort reaches before turning cash-positive.
  • C8 — Year-1 DE floor (default −$150M): the 2026 (first-year) DE must not be more negative than the floor — an explicit cap on first-year acquisition strain, which also acts as a capital-budget proxy on how aggressive a single-year mix can be. Baseline year-1 DE is about −$109M, so the default leaves ~$41M of headroom. It has its own panel on the Constraint Evidence tab and a row in the What-if shadow prices.
  • C9 — Trough RBC tail (x = 350%, y = 25%) — Slow mode only: across the stochastic draws, the probability that a scenario's trough RBC ratio (minimum over 2026–2030, full book, note-adjusted) falls below x must be ≤ y. The stochastic counterpart to C1: where C1 caps the deterministic trough, this caps how often capital adequacy breaches under shocks. It requires a full RBC recompute on every draw, so it is evaluated only when the run is set to Slow mode (~4–5 min at 100×100); Fast runs skip it.
6 · Frontier & Stochastic Method

Scenario sampling. Sales levels are drawn by Latin Hypercube Sampling across the configured bounds for each product, giving even coverage of the sales space with relatively few scenarios. The stochastic draw count is floored at 50 (default 100); the C4 tail test counts exceedances among these draws, so too few draws make feasibility unstable. Runs are seeded, so a given configuration reproduces the same frontier; the seed is set in the Configuration tab (Randomize draws a fresh one).

Deterministic axis (return). Each scenario's plotted PVDE is the discounted 2026-issue DE stream (all three products). The IRR shown is the 2026-issue IRR; the target is the 2026-weighted hurdle.

Stochastic axis (risk). For each scenario, the claims and termination rates are perturbed by lognormal shocks (per the σ table) and the 2026-issue PVDE is recomputed for each draw. Each shock has two parts: a systematic component (one persistent draw applied to every year) and a process component (an independent draw each year), combined as a mean-1 lognormal multiplier per year. Preneed is the exception: its claims and termination are one coupled mortality shock (a death is at once a claim, a reserve release and a decrement), and it carries an additional Preneed-only NIER (investment-yield) shock — an additive basis-point shift on the earned rate — which is its dominant risk. See Risk Calibration below. Systematic risk drives the tail because it never diversifies; process risk largely averages out over the 30-year horizon. To make scenarios comparable, every scenario is evaluated against the same shared bank of draws (common random numbers), each draw paired with its mirror (antithetic), so differences between scenarios reflect the sales mix rather than Monte-Carlo noise. The scenario's risk is the CTE-90 downside shortfall — how much 2026 PVDE the worst 10% of outcomes give up versus the deterministic plan (lower is better). The table and tooltip also report the worst-decile max drawdown (deepest cumulative-DE point across draws); std-dev and downside semi-deviation are retained in the CSV export for reference. The P10 IRR is the 10th percentile of the 2026-issue IRR across draws. See the Risk Calibration sections below for how the σ inputs are derived.

Efficient frontier. Among feasible scenarios, a scenario is on the frontier if no other feasible scenario has both higher PVDE and lower-or-equal risk (and is strictly better on at least one). The min-RBC statistic reported alongside remains a full-book, all-issue-year figure.

7 · Modeling Notes & Conventions
  • Horizon & month indexing: months 0–359 (Dec 2025 – Nov 2055). The annual rollup of flows matches the workbook's VNB Recalc month-to-year mapping. The final calendar month (Dec 2055) is intentionally cut off in both the VNB Calc and VNB Recalc — it errors out the model transformations — so calendar 2055 contains 11 months (Jan–Nov).
  • Preneed "change in loading": Preneed carries a change-in-loading line where the other products carry ceded reserves. This is a source-data difference, not a modeling inconsistency, and the term is included in Preneed's other-benefits cost.
  • Original-sales anchors: the scenario sales scalar divides by the workbook's per-year original-sales anchor for each issue year. Anchors are carried and editable for all scalar years (2026–2035).
  • Validation: with the workbook's own scenario scalars, recalc IRRs reconcile to the source VNB Recalc within a few basis points per product (MS and HI to the basis point); per-cohort lives reconcile to the penny.

Risk Calibration

Risk Calibration — what the σ inputs really represent

The stochastic engine multiplies each product's incurred claims and its termination rate by a lognormal shock with mean 1 and standard deviation σ. So σ is not the volatility of an individual claim — it is the volatility of the aggregate annual actual-to-expected (A/E) ratio for the whole block: the chance that, in a given year, total claims (or total terminations) come in materially above or below what the EV projection assumes. That is exactly the quantity the frontier's risk axis prices.

The "lapse" σ is an all-cause termination σ. The decrement these shocks scale is total termination — voluntary lapse plus mortality plus other exits — not voluntary lapse alone. That matters most for Medicare Supplement and Hospital Indemnity, where terminations mix surrender and mortality.

Preneed is modeled differently — claims and termination are ONE shock. Preneed is pre-funded, so a death is simultaneously the claim, the reserve release, and the decrement. Shocking termination on its own (or gross claims on their own) is therefore wrong. Preneed uses a single mortality shock that drives all three together: more deaths raise claims, remove lives, and release those lives' reserves, with the strain netting to the net amount at risk (benefit − reserve) — large for young business where reserves are still small, shrinking as the block matures. Because the two are one event, Preneed has no separate termination σ and no claims↔termination ρ; its claims σ is the mortality σ. Preneed's dominant risk is instead the NIER (investment-yield) shock — see its calibration below.

These values are calibrated to published industry experience as a defensible starting bracket. They are informed priors, not final assumptions; the appropriate basis is the carrier's own seriatim A/E history (see methodology and limitations).

Recommended σ — systematic and process components

Each annual A/E multiplier carries two independent pieces: a systematic shock (a persistent assumption/pricing miss — one draw held across all 30 years) and a process shock (random year-to-year fluctuation — a fresh draw each year). They combine as mult(y) = exp(zsys·σsys + zproc(y)·σproc − ½(σsys²+σproc²)), mean 1.

The systematic component dominates every line and drives the frontier's tail risk, because a persistent miss never cancels while year-to-year process noise largely averages out over the 30-year horizon. Setting process to 0 recovers a pure single-shock model. σ calibrates how risk is measured; feasibility is governed by the constraint suite.

Why split systematic from process

Actuarial credibility theory decomposes the total variance of experience into process variance — the inherent randomness of the claim/decrement process, which diversifies away as exposure grows — and parameter (structure) variance, the variance of the true underlying means, which does not diversify. The persistent assumption miss is parameter risk; the year-to-year wobble is process risk.

The magnitude of the process piece is anchored by the full-credibility standard: a block large enough to be fully credible has an annual aggregate-claims process coefficient of variation of roughly 1–2.5%, with smaller or partially-credible blocks running higher. That is why process σ is set low for the large Medicare Supplement and Preneed blocks (2–3%) and higher for the smaller Hospital Indemnity block (4%).

It also explains the split of the original calibration. The year-to-year loss-ratio swings the σ values were drawn from (e.g. Medigap ~72–85% over recent years) are the total variation, which already contained a process slice. Holding the combined annual σ at those observed levels and carving out the credibility-based process floor leaves the systematic component as √(total² − process²) — slightly below the former single number, because that single number had been treating diversifiable noise as if it persisted for 30 years. The combined σ is unchanged; only its interpretation is sharper.

Process correlation (claims ↔ termination)

The systematic shocks stay independent across risks and products — a pricing miss on claims is its own event. The process shocks, however, are correlated within a product and year: a year of unexpectedly heavy claims tends to coincide with unexpectedly heavy termination. Each year’s lapse-process draw is built as zlapse = ρ·zclaims + √(1−ρ²)·zindep, so ρ sets how tightly the two move together (0 = independent). Correlating the draws raises portfolio tail risk because the two adverse events stack rather than diversify.

Why positive. The link is antiselection: when more policyholders terminate, the healthier lives tend to leave and the sicker pool that remains carries higher claims per life. SOA/SCOR post-level-term studies document this directly — the same drivers that raise shock lapses also raise mortality (and morbidity) deterioration.

Default ρ by product (editable in Configuration): Medicare Supplement 0.25 and Hospital Indemnity 0.25 (moderate antiselective coupling). Preneed is excluded — its claims and termination are not merely correlated, they are the same mortality event, so they are modeled as one coupled shock (ρ = 1 by construction) rather than via a tunable ρ. These are deliberately modest pending the carrier’s own joint claims/lapse experience.

Preneed NIER (investment-yield) shock — the dominant PN risk

Preneed is a long-duration, pre-funded block: a large reserve is held for decades and earns investment income at the net investment earned rate (NIER). The new-business margin is thin and highly levered to that earned rate, so a miss on NIER is the single largest risk on the block — larger than the mortality shock. The model therefore applies a Preneed-only NIER shock: an additive basis-point level shift to the earned rate (not a lognormal multiplier), split into a systematic piece (a persistent miss on the long-run earned rate, held across all years) and a process piece (year-to-year reinvestment fluctuation). An adverse draw lowers the rate, reducing investment income and distributable earnings; the shock is mean-zero and symmetric. On the risk axis it feeds the same 2026-issue PVDE the frontier prices, alongside the mortality shock. Scoping (which cohorts it touches): the systematic (pricing) miss applies to business priced from 2026 forward; the process (reinvestment) fluctuation applies to every issue year, including the pre-2026 in-force. That full-book difference is routed into the RBC / TAC calculation — when you select a stochastic run on the RBC or Debug tab, the displayed required-capital and TAC reflect the NIER stress on the whole block (back book on process; new business on systematic+process). Feasibility and the chart's risk axis stay 2026-issue. (The per-product 2026-issue IRR is unaffected by NIER scoping and remains scale-invariant — see the Debug tab.)

Calibration (default 35 bps systematic / 15 bps process). The earned rate here is a portfolio book yield, which moves slowly because only new-money reinvestment repriced at current rates flows through each year. Industry evidence:

  • U.S. life-industry net yield on invested assets ≈ 4.57% at year-end 2024, with year-over-year book-yield moves of roughly 15 bps (fixed-income book yield +15 bps in 2024; net yield +25/+26 bps in 2024/2023; a ~60 bps drift over the four years 2018–2022, ≈ 15 bps/yr). This anchors the process σ at ~15 bps.
  • Preneed pricing and valuation carry interest/discount-rate adverse-deviation margins of 20–50 bps (per preneed insurer SEC filings) — the provision actuaries hold for a sustained earned-rate miss. This, together with the multi-year drift above, anchors the systematic σ at ~35 bps.

Sources: NEAM, 2024 U.S. Life Industry Investment Highlights; NAIC, 2024 Annual Life Industry Commentary and capital-markets asset-mix report (YE2024); preneed insurer SEC filings (interest/discount-rate adverse-deviation 0.20–0.50%). Both σ values are editable in the Configuration tab; set process to 0 for a pure level-shift model.

Evidence by product

Medicare Supplement — claims (moderate, mostly systematic). Industry Medigap loss ratios swung roughly from the low-70s% to the mid-80s% over recent years (≈80% in 2019, ≈72% in the 2020 pandemic year, ≈77% in 2021, ≈85% individual by 2024 per NAIC experience exhibits and Telos/RGA analyses). Excluding the pandemic outlier, the year-to-year spread implies a coefficient of variation on the annual loss ratio of ~4–6%. Because the line is large, annually-repriced and minimum-loss-ratio-regulated, process risk is small and the volatility is trend/rate-cycle driven — supporting a claims σ near 5%.

Medicare Supplement — termination. Medigap termination is structurally rate-sensitive. Premiums rise every year with no federal cap on increases, which drives switching, and a widening set of states force annual switching windows — California and Oregon birthday rules, Virginia's birthday rule effective July 2025, and year-round guaranteed-issue in CT, ME, MA and NY. Guaranteed-issue lets healthier lives leave when rates rise, so terminations both spike and antiselect in step with the rate cycle. Because these are 65+ lives, a meaningful and relatively steady slice of all-cause termination is mortality, which dilutes the σ of the total termination rate. Net of those forces, an all-cause termination σ around 7% is defensible.

Preneed — the most stable line. Preneed is effectively single- or limited-pay whole life, so claims are mortality, and aggregate mortality A/E on a sizeable block is stable (insurer LDTI disclosures show whole-life mortality A/E near 0.9–1.0), supporting a claims σ around 4%. Because most industry preneed is single-pay and barely surrenders, all-cause termination is dominated by mortality — so its σ is low (≈5%) and largely the same event as the claims shock. The watch-items are anti-selection at issue and benefit-side funeral-cost inflation.

Hospital Indemnity — bounded by the indemnity structure. Hospital indemnity pays fixed, scheduled benefits (a set amount per hospital day or event) rather than reimbursing actual medical cost. That caps benefit exposure per claim, so utilization variance translates into bounded benefit variance — materially less volatile than the open-ended reimbursement morbidity behind the largest supplemental-health A/E swings (a recent insurer LDTI filing showed morbidity 23–29% above expected on a reimbursement-style block). Morbidity remains the most variable of the three claims drivers, so HI claims σ is kept highest of the lines at ~7%, while the indemnity benefit structure holds it well below open-ended morbidity. Termination is individual senior business — rate-driven lapse plus mortality, not worksite churn — supporting ≈8%.

Caveats & what would sharpen these numbers
  • These σ are systematic-risk, portfolio-level figures appropriate for sizing risk on large 2026 sales plans. They deliberately do not credit a diversification (volume) benefit; if the seriatim decomposition shows non-trivial process risk, large scenarios could earn a small σ reduction.
  • Each shock is a two-component draw — a persistent systematic piece held across the projection plus an independent process piece redrawn each year — and within a product the process claims and termination draws are correlated via ρ (Preneed is one coupled mortality shock). The remaining refinements are letting the process piece mean-revert/trend (autocorrelation) and adding correlation across products; both are on the roadmap.
  • σ does not set feasibility. The feasible region is governed by the constraint suite — in particular the C4 IRR-tail test — not by these σ directly. These figures should be replaced with carrier-specific A/E calibration once the seriatim series is built.

Limitations & roadmap

The model is deliberately scoped. The items below are documented gaps and planned refinements; none affect the deterministic engine, which reconciles to the workbook.

Methodology extensions

  • Own-data σ calibration. Replace the industry-derived claims and termination σ priors with Wellabe's seriatim actual-to-expected history — the single highest-value refinement. Method:
    1. Build an annual A/E series per product — actual incurred claims ÷ expected, and actual all-cause terminations ÷ expected, each calendar year (15+ years gives a usable sample).
    2. Decompose process vs. systematic risk — process risk shrinks ~1/√n with volume; systematic risk (trend, rate-cycle, utilization) does not. For sizing large sales plans, keep mostly the systematic residual (total A/E variance minus expected process variance).
    3. Don't assume normal — fit it — A/E ratios are right-skewed and bounded at zero (lognormal is the right family); confirm with a block bootstrap (resample whole calendar years) and a Q–Q plot, swapping to empirical-quantile draws if the tails are fatter.
    4. Calibrate termination on early durations — the frontier isolates the 2026 cohort, so early-duration (1–3) termination volatility dominates.
    5. Measure the claims↔termination correlation from paired annual residuals, per product, to set ρ (Preneed is effectively one event; MS rate increases couple the two).
  • Cross-product correlation. The process shocks now couple claims and termination within each product (see Risk Calibration). Still independent across products — MS and HI claims share utilization drivers, so a common-shock or copula layer across product lines would further shape the portfolio tail. Systematic shocks remain independent throughout.
  • Within-process autocorrelation / trend (refinement). The two-component shock — a persistent systematic draw plus an independent annual process draw — is built in (see Risk Calibration). The remaining refinement is to let the process component mean-revert or trend year to year (an AR-type process) rather than be independent across years, which would shape the medium-horizon tail.
  • Reinsurance as a lever. A quota-share or other treaty changes capital strain and risk per unit of new business, shifting the efficient frontier and potentially making otherwise-infeasible mixes reachable. Integrating the companion reinsurance tool's economics as a toggle is the next major build.
  • Multi-year optimization. The frontier optimizes 2026 in isolation; 2026 is the first move in a 2026–2035 trajectory, and the surplus-note and RBC paths make years interdependent. Planned: test the 2026 optimum for robustness across forward-year plans, then optimize the path.
  • Smoothed C4 tail estimator. Estimate the IRR-tail probability by fitting a distribution to the draws and integrating, rather than counting exceedances, to remove small-sample lumpiness near the threshold.

Validation / QA

  • Preneed cohort reconciliation. Trace a handful of PN cells (a 2026 cohort and the <2026 block) back to the source workbook and add unit tests on the divide-by-zero guard branches.
  • Convention confirmations. Confirm the embedded RBC factor (the ×1.03 required-capital multiplier) and the cumulative TAC roll-forward (seed pre-tax income delta; per-year pre-tax/after-tax switch on whether baseline TAC declined) against the source workbook.

Business inputs to confirm

  • 2026 sales bounds. Confirm the per-product sampling ranges reflect realistic 2026 distribution capacity, so the frontier explores the achievable opportunity set.

Considered — closed

  • Capital-budget sampling. The data-science concern that the sampler draws products independently rather than within a shared capital budget is addressed by the Year-1 DE floor constraint, which caps first-year capital strain and gates over-aggressive combinations.
  • NPV / IRR timing. The one-period offset between the NPV (end-of-year) and IRR (t=0) conventions is intentional Excel-faithful behavior; confirmed acceptable.
